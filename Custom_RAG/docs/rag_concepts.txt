RAG: Retrieval, Metadata Filtering, and Chunking
==================================================

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) enhances LLM responses by retrieving relevant information from an external knowledge base before generating an answer. This document covers three critical RAG components in depth: retrieval strategies, metadata filtering, and chunking techniques.

Retrieval Strategies
--------------------

Retrieval is the process of finding the most relevant documents or passages for a given query. The quality of retrieval directly impacts the quality of the final answer.

Dense Retrieval (Semantic Search):
- Uses embedding models to convert text into dense vectors.
- Finds similar documents by measuring cosine similarity or dot product between query and document vectors.
- Captures semantic meaning — "happy" and "joyful" would have similar embeddings even though the words are different.
- Common embedding models: OpenAI text-embedding-3-small, Cohere embed-v3, Sentence-BERT.

Sparse Retrieval (Keyword Search):
- Uses traditional methods like BM25 or TF-IDF.
- Matches based on exact keyword overlap.
- Works well when exact terminology matters (e.g., product codes, legal terms).
- Fast and interpretable but misses semantic relationships.

Hybrid Retrieval:
- Combines dense and sparse retrieval using Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) or weighted scoring.
- Gets the best of both worlds: semantic understanding plus keyword precision.
- Increasingly common in production RAG systems.

Re-Ranking:
- After initial retrieval, a cross-encoder model re-scores each candidate document against the query.
- More computationally expensive but significantly improves relevance.
- Popular re-rankers: Cohere Rerank, cross-encoder models from Hugging Face.

Metadata Filtering
------------------

Metadata filtering allows you to narrow down search results based on structured attributes attached to documents, before or during the similarity search.

Common metadata fields:
- source: The original document name or URL.
- date: When the document was created or last updated.
- category: Topic, department, or section classification.
- author: Who wrote the document.
- chunk_index: Position of the chunk within the source document.
- language: Language of the document.

How metadata filtering works in ChromaDB:
When querying ChromaDB, you can pass a `where` filter:
  collection.query(
      query_embeddings=[embedding],
      n_results=5,
      where={"category": "technical"},
      where_document={"$contains": "python"}
  )

Benefits of metadata filtering:
- Reduces search space, improving both speed and relevance.
- Enables multi-tenant RAG systems where each user only sees their own documents.
- Allows time-based filtering to prioritize recent information.
- Supports category-scoped searches (e.g., only search HR policies, not engineering docs).

Chunking Strategies
-------------------

Chunking is the process of breaking large documents into smaller pieces for embedding and retrieval. The chunking strategy significantly affects retrieval quality.

Fixed-Size Chunking:
- Splits text into chunks of a fixed character or token count (e.g., 400 characters).
- Simple to implement and works well for uniform content.
- May split sentences or paragraphs awkwardly.
- Adding overlap (e.g., 80 characters) between chunks helps preserve context across boundaries.

Sentence-Based Chunking:
- Splits text at sentence boundaries using NLP sentence tokenizers.
- Preserves grammatical coherence within each chunk.
- Chunk sizes can vary significantly.

Paragraph-Based Chunking:
- Splits on paragraph or section boundaries (e.g., double newlines or headers).
- Preserves topical coherence — each chunk is about one idea or subtopic.
- Best for well-structured documents.

Semantic Chunking:
- Uses embeddings to group semantically related sentences together.
- Sentences are split into a new chunk when the embedding similarity between consecutive sentences drops below a threshold.
- Produces the most coherent chunks but is computationally expensive.

Recursive Chunking:
- Tries to split on the largest meaningful boundary first (e.g., sections), then paragraphs, then sentences, then characters.
- Used by LangChain's RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter.
- Balances coherence and consistent chunk size.

Chunking Best Practices:
- Aim for chunk sizes of 200-1000 tokens depending on your embedding model's context window.
- Always include overlap (10-20% of chunk size) to avoid losing context at boundaries.
- Attach metadata (source, section title, page number) to each chunk for traceability.
- Test different strategies and measure retrieval quality on your specific data.
- Consider using parent-child chunking: store small chunks for retrieval but return the larger parent context to the LLM.
